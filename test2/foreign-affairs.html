The Effect of Foreign Affairs on the American Perception

While many Americans had perceived their nation as the primary propagator of democratic ideals and human rights, ultimately foreign intervention resulted in political disasters and duplicity, all of which created an enmity and distrust towards the American government, whereas foreign assaults against America created crises and tragedy which unified Americans and rallied support for the government.

9/11

Overview:
On September 11 of 2001, Al-Qaeda terrorists hijacked 4 United States airplanes and and assaulted many US institutions, such as the World Trade Center and Pentagon. This attack was undeniably changed America – from more intensive security measures to prevent a future attack to a war directed against terrorism in the subsequent Iraq War. Nations from all around the world offered the United States their condolences and condemned the acts of terrorism by the radical Muslim jihadists. There is one theme is common among all Americans – that day unified Americans across the globe and the memories associated with this tragic event will not become forgotten in the flow of history.
News reports to reflect reactions of Americans during the terrorist attacks of 9/11.
Synthesis:
Due to the psychologically taxing nature of 9/11, many Americans remembered exactly what they were doing while receiving the news of the terrorist attack.While she was just arriving to school to teach her class, Ms. Mia Onodera learned of the terrorist attacks on 9/11 and recalls a state of shock. “There was just confusion and no one knew exactly what was going on” she says, citing the conflicting news reports that were initially imparted during the early phases of the attack. Afterwards however, one thing was certain, she knew that America would be forever changed, and whether the subsequent Patriot Act of 2001 was justified in an exchange of personal liberties for national security is a matter of contentious debate, to which Mr. Preston Wong states “I think that the events of 9/11 occur very rarely… in comparison to how regularly citizens of this country [need to] exercise their rights…”.

​This difference in opinion in regards to the aftermath of the September 11th attacks is by no means an unanimous accord, but the reaction from all Americans, regardless of demographics, was that of unity and compassion. As Mr. Albert Boledo puts it, if there were one virtue to be gained by the whole ordeal of 9/11, it would be the “unifying sense that bonded the entire nation” and desire to help the victims of 9/11 which pushed America past this crisis and advanced the nation’s democratic ideals. The resolution shown by all Americans during and after 9/11 reaffirms the American spirit to many and serves as an emblem of the resilience that Americans have come to respect throughout history.

Cold War: Fear of Communism

Overview:
Nearly right after the close of WWII, the US entered a protracted and indirect war with the Soviet Union, the only other superpower at that time. From space research to proxy wars to the nuclear arms race, the US battled against the Soviet Union, with propaganda on both sides deriding the central ideals of that nation. This sharp contrast between the fundamental ideologies of both countries prompted fears that there were communist spies in America. This fear, further perpetuated by the iron curtain set by the Soviet Union and the proxy wars fought over territorial influence, prompted the rise of an anti-communist surge in America, led by Senator McCarthy, that included suspicion amongst citizens of each other.
American cartoon during the Cold War deriding communism.
﻿Synthesis:
The Red Scare was a very real fear during the 1950s, in which demagogues, most primarily Senator McCarthy, capitalized on the fears of communism from many Americans in order to arrest his political opponents. A more personal narrative on this example, Mr. Albert Boledo recalls how certain movies from Hollywood were “blacklisted”, or were completely omitted from the public due to their possible communist influences. In fact, the United States government during this time suspected that Hollywood was the main propagator of the communist party’s agenda, and thus led heavy inspections into the actors and screenwriters, many of whom were wrongly accused and had their artwork tampered with.

​Mr. Albert Boledo goes on to say how this whole deal of “government-supported communist fear” encouraged increased distrust with the US government from the individuals at this time. Although she did not remember much of this time period, Mrs. Irene Wong recalls how her mother bought into the US anti-communist propaganda and displayed complete distrust in anything associated with communism, including her heritage in Mainland China, which was under communist rule. Overall, a common theme echoed throughout this fear of communism epitomizes the growing distrust Americans contained towards their government, a sentiment which would goad the Americans to a near-crisis situation, especially during the Vietnam War and the leak of the Pentagon Papers.

Vietnam War

﻿Overview:
An offset of the ongoing Cold War, the Vietnam War was a matter of contentious debate amongst Americans living at that time. Prior to the release of the infamous Pentagon Papers, which detailed the questionable motives for the American involvement in the war, American citizens nearly unanimously acquiesced with the government’s intent to go to war against North Vietnam. However, upon learning of devastation and cover-ups employed by the press and government institutions in Vietnam, many Americans disapproved of their country’s involvement and contributed to the anti-war sentiment throughout America, a sentiment often propagated through student protests and sit-ins.
Devastating effects of "Agent Orange" (Napalm) in Vietnam War.
﻿Synthesis:
The Vietnam War engendered the rancor of many Americans, especially since this was the first time an army draft was effected on such an expansive level, often forcing individuals in their late-teens to early 20s into the midst of a foreign country without a clear objective. Mr. Albert Boledo, just out of the draft age at this time, recalls the fear of the draft that hung over the young Americans. “Getting drafted in the [United States] Army during this time… well, some would definitely try to escape than face duty… especially in a Vietnam, a land [in which] who knows what can happen”. A sentiment not unique to himself, the popularity of the Vietnam War sharply declined, especially as Americans realized their prolonged stay in Vietnam was costing many American and Vietnamese lives for no clear objective, a realization further perpetuated by North Vietnamese resilience (especially during the Tet Offensive) and the infamous Pentagon Papers, which revealed that despite the lies the government had told the public, the Americans had actually intentionally initiated the war on Vietnam, which it considered an offset of the communist uprisings around the globe. This caused a complete lack of trust between the American public and the government, a divide that is still prevalent and has hardly improved today.
