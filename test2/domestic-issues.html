The Effect of Domestic Issues on American Reslience

Through crises and difficulties that affected every fiber of American social and political life, Americans were resilient in their efforts to build a more ideal nation; it is often through these situations which America rebuilds itself stronger than before.

Civil Rights

Overview:
Especially during the 1960s, civil rights activists pushed for legislative change to empower African Americans, and often met significant challenges, ranging from violent segregationists to stagnant bureaucracy. The passage of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 marked a major cornerstone for the success of the classic phase of the Civil Rights Movement; however, the shift from nonviolent direct action to black-empowerment centered protests led to the first urban riots that captured the attention of the whole nation, leaving memorable and controversial reactions from the individuals during this generation.
Dr. Martin Luther King and Birmingham violence towards protestors.
Synthesis:
The Civil Rights Movement irrefutably affected the fiber of American society, especially during the 1950s and 1960s, in which significant legislation and demonstrations occurred in an effort for the African Americans to gain attention to their cause. Mr. Albert Boledo remembers seeing on the news how many Black activists were “violently rewarded” by the police and the institutions that were supposed to protect all Americans. According to Mr. Boledo, seeing these graphic videos of brutality on the television of fellow Americans beat each other up over skin color was what caused him to “change [his] perception of what should be fair to all mankind”. After these demonstrations specifically created to attract media attention and promote legislative success, the Civil Rights Movement continued all throughout the 20th century and is still a hotly contended subject today. Mr. Scott Deruiter agrees that there has been significant progress in terms of civil rights for all people regardless of skin color and more recently, sexuality, and thinks that as America continues to become more liberal, these civil rights will eventually become more settled for all Americans.

​Another perspective is that of Mr. Preston Wong’s, he sees the progress of civil rights as definitely beneficial to the American public as a whole; however, given the more violent and riotous nature of more current civil rights activism, such as the Black Lives Matter movement, Mr. Wong questions to what extent are civil rights activism actually beneficial to its proponents as opposed to creating more difficulties to society than good. Overall, it is clear that civil rights have been steadily improving – from the early nonviolent protests of the early 1950s to the inauguration of Barack Obama, an African American, as the president of the United States.

NASA Challenger Explosion

Overview:
Created in the midst of the Cold War in 1958, NASA’s objective to explore the vast mysteries of outer space captivated the imaginations of many Americans. However on January 28, 1986, that enthrallment with NASA quickly turned sour as the American orbiter Challenger exploded shortly after takeoff, killing the entire crew including Christa McAuliffe, a high school teacher. The tragedy is attributed to the cold conditions during takeoff, and this event unified the American people – galvanizing them to bond together to overcome these sad times.
NASA Challenger explosion live on CNN.
Synthesis:
In 1958, Americans watched in horror as the NASA Challenger spacecraft exploded while carrying its crew into space due to faulty gear. Since the Challenger spacecraft had passed all safety requirements, although reports that the cold temperature could possibly cause certain elements of the spacecraft the malfunction were ignored by the superiors, many Americans waited in anticipation for the spacecraft to take off and journey into the outer realms of the known world. That all changed when the spacecraft exploded around 70 seconds into the air.

​Reflecting on this incident, Ms. Mia Onodera states that the most poignant aspect of the accident was how “there was a teacher on-board the Challenger spacecraft, so that pretty much shocked everyone into how could this disaster have happened… this is the United States government who thought the mission [was safe enough to bring a teacher onboard]”. Combined with the civil rights protests during this time, suggesting a possible government disinterest towards domestic concerns as well as increasing foreign intervention policies with questionable objectives, the Challenger Explosion perpetuated a distrust and contempt towards the American government. However, another message is echoed, as the tragedy unfolded, many Americans gathered and offered their solitude to those who had died aboard the Challenger spacecraft. The spirit of community that follows national catastrophe and misfortune is in the hearts of all Americans, regardless of generation, and will continue to serve as a form of compassion for those who have done what they could for the nation.

Assassination of JFK and MLK

﻿Overview:
The 1960s was a turbulent time in which social change was often met with violent opposition that sent shockwaves across the nation. In particular, the high-profile assassinations of President John F. Kennedy and Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. triggered a massive reaction across the American public, from a commitment to follow through on Kennedy’s civil rights legislation plans to urban rioting in nearly every major city across the United States after the death of King. These events and subsequent consequences prompted the American public to realize the social unrest previously concealed and commit to a higher order of social change.
Synthesis:
The deaths of popularized leaders, as were President John F. Kennedy and Dr. Martin Luther King Jr., sparked waves of outrage amongst Americans – violence had overturned America’s progress and contributed to the decline of moral standards. This was a time of crisis for many Americans, as they watched their leaders in both the political and social realm meet their unfortunate fate, and as Mr. Albert Boledo puts it, “[The assassination of JFK] made many Americans realize that this great leader was just man and is  as easily-broken as anyone else”. The speciality of these leaders is the virtues they attribute themselves to – as Kennedy puts it “ask not what your country can do for you — ask what you can do for your country” (President John F. Kennedy).

​Reflecting on how the assassination of Dr. King, Mr. Albert Boledo remembers seeing the news of the LA riots (he was living in California during this time), and the anger he too felt towards the person who had murdered Dr. King. However, Mr. Boledo criticizes the riots, suggesting that using violence as a means to channel the hatred felt towards America is an inappropriate response and results in no benefits to be accrued by any party involved. On the whole, the general sentiment of Americans after these unfortunate casualties was that of despair – what America could have become had these men not been assassinated is a realization that still haunts America today.
